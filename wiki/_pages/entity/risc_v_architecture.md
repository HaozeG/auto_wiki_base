# RISC-V Architecture

RISC-V is an open-source instruction set architecture (ISA) based on reduced instruction set computing (RISC) principles. Originally developed at the University of California, Berkeley, in 2010, RISC-V has evolved into a global standard maintained by the nonprofit RISC-V International. Its key differentiator from proprietary ISAs like ARM and x86 is its open and royalty-free licensing model, which allows any entity to design, manufacture, and sell chips without paying licensing fees. By 2026, RISC-V has become a significant disruptor in the chip industry, challenging the dominance of ARM in embedded systems and mobile devices, and Intel's x86 in data centers and personal computing. The architecture's openness has also made it a focal point in geopolitical debates over semiconductor sovereignty, particularly as Chinese companies have adopted RISC-V to reduce dependence on foreign chip designs amid US export controls on advanced semiconductors. RISC-V's modular design includes optional extensions for vector processing, machine learning, and cryptography, making it adaptable across a wide range of applications from IoT to high-performance computing.

## Key Claims

- RISC-V is an open-source ISA developed and maintained by RISC-V International through its technical working groups; the core base ISA and standard extensions are released without royalty obligations.
- Geopolitically, RISC-V has become a focal point in debates over semiconductor sovereignty and export control, with some US policymakers raising concerns about Chinese dominance in the RISC-V ecosystem.
- ARM Holdings experienced a 4.9% stock decline, partly attributed to investor unease about ARM's ability to defend its market share against RISC-V alternatives.
- Chinese companies have taken a leading role in RISC-V development, leveraging the open architecture to circumvent restrictions on proprietary US and UK chip designs.
- In the embedded systems market, RISC-V is emerging as a credible strategic alternative to ARM, which has long dominated that segment.
- RISC-V's modular extension framework allows customization for specialized workloads, including custom coprocessors and accelerators.
