# Tesla Dojo

Tesla Dojo is a series of supercomputers designed and built by Tesla Inc. for training computer vision and machine learning models, particularly for Tesla's Full Self-Driving (FSD) advanced driver-assistance system. The project was announced in 2019 and officially entered production in July 2023. Dojo is built around custom D1 chips, which are fabricated on TSMC's 7nm process and contain 50 billion transistors on a 645 mm² die. The architecture emphasizes scalability by relying on a distributed SRAM mesh instead of traditional cache coherence mechanisms, enabling efficient processing of massive video datasets captured from Tesla's vehicle fleet. The system supports custom numeric formats including CFloat8 and CFloat16 for optimized AI training. The Dojo project was reported disbanded in August 2025 but restarted in January 2026 with a new chip iteration.

## Key Claims

- The D1 chip is manufactured on TSMC's 7nm process with 50 billion transistors and a die size of 645 mm².
- Dojo's architecture uses a distributed SRAM mesh to achieve high scalability and interconnect bandwidth.
- Custom Configurable Float8 (CFloat8) and Configurable Float16 (CFloat16) formats are defined as extensions of IEEE 754.
- In testing, a single Dojo cabinet drew 2.3 MW and tripped a San Jose power substation.
- Future computing clusters at Gigafactory Texas are planned with total thermal design power of 130 MW scaling to 500 MW.
- The earlier Tesla GPU cluster (Nvidia A100) claimed 81.6 petaflops, though this performance benchmark is disputed.
- Dojo production began in July 2023; the project was disbanded in August 2025 and restarted in January 2026.
