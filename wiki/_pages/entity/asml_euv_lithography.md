# ASML EUV Lithography

Extreme Ultraviolet (EUV) lithography is the patterning technology that enables production of advanced semiconductor nodes at 7 nm and below. ASML, a Dutch company headquartered in Veldhoven, Netherlands, is the sole global manufacturer of EUV lithography machines, giving it a 100% market share in this critical equipment segment. EUV tools use 13.5 nm wavelength light — generated by firing high-powered lasers at tin droplets to create plasma — to etch circuit features onto silicon wafers with precision impossible at longer wavelengths used in older deep-ultraviolet (DUV) tools. The flagship EUV system, the NXE series (e.g., NXE:3400C), achieves a throughput of approximately 170 wafers per hour and costs roughly $150–200 million per machine. A next-generation platform, High-NA EUV (numerical aperture 0.55, versus 0.33 NA on conventional EUV), entered customer evaluation in 2024 under the model name EXE:5000 and EXE:5200, priced at approximately $350–380 million per unit. High-NA EUV enables single-exposure patterning of features below 8 nm half-pitch, targeting the A14 (Angstrom-class) nodes planned by TSMC and Intel for 2025–2027. Without EUV or High-NA EUV, chipmakers must rely on costly and imprecise multi-patterning with DUV tools, increasing process steps and yield risk. ASML's monopoly position, combined with the machine's export-controlled status, makes it a chokepoint in global AI chip supply chains.

## Key Claims

- ASML holds 100% market share in EUV lithography tools; no other company has shipped a commercial EUV system.
- Conventional EUV machines (NXE series, 0.33 NA) cost approximately $150–200 million each and produce roughly 170 wafers per hour.
- High-NA EUV machines (EXE series, 0.55 NA) cost approximately $350–380 million per unit and began customer deliveries in 2024, with Intel receiving the first EXE:5200 system.
- EUV light is produced at 13.5 nm wavelength via laser-produced plasma from tin droplets, requiring vacuum optical paths because EUV is absorbed by air.
- The Dutch government, under pressure from the United States, restricted ASML from exporting EUV machines to China starting in 2019; as of 2023 DUV restrictions were further tightened, blocking SMIC and other Chinese fabs from accessing leading-edge EUV tools.
- TSMC's N3 and N2 process nodes require multiple EUV exposure layers per die; N2 (targeting 2025 volume production) uses EUV for a greater fraction of critical layers than N3.
- High-NA EUV is required for sub-8 nm half-pitch features without multi-patterning, enabling TSMC's A14 and Intel's 14A nodes targeting post-2026 production.
